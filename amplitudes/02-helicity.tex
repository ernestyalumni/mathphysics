\chapter{Helicity States and Weyl Spinors}
\label{ch:helicity}

This chapter builds the spinor algebra that underlies everything in
Parts~II and~III.  The physical target is simple: massless particles carry
definite \emph{helicity} $h = \pm\tfrac{1}{2}$ (or $\pm 1$ for gluons), and
the two-component Weyl spinors $\lambda_\alpha$ and $\tilde\lambda_{\dot\alpha}$
are precisely the objects that represent those helicity eigenstates.
We establish this connection carefully here so that Chapter~\ref{ch:spinor-helicity}
can use the spinor-bracket formalism without re-deriving its foundations.

%% ============================================================
\section{Lorentz Group Representations}
%% ============================================================

The Lorentz algebra $\mathfrak{so}(1,3)$ is (complexified) isomorphic to
$\mathfrak{su}(2)_L \oplus \mathfrak{su}(2)_R$ via the non-Hermitian combinations
\begin{align}
  N_i &\equiv \tfrac{1}{2}(J_i - iK_i), &
  N_i^\dagger &\equiv \tfrac{1}{2}(J_i + iK_i),
\end{align}
where $J_i$ are rotation generators and $K_i$ are boost generators.
They satisfy $[N_i, N_j] = i\varepsilon_{ijk}N_k$,
$[N_i^\dagger, N_j^\dagger] = i\varepsilon_{ijk}N_k^\dagger$,
$[N_i, N_j^\dagger] = 0$, so irreducible representations are labelled
by two half-integers $(n,\, n')$.

\medskip
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccc@{}}
  \toprule
  $(n,n')$ & Srednicki & Dim & Field & Index type \\
  \midrule
  $(0,0)$ & $(1,1)$ & 1 & scalar $\phi$ & none \\
  $(\tfrac{1}{2},0)$ & $(2,1)$ & 2 & left-handed Weyl $\psi_\alpha$ & undotted \\
  $(0,\tfrac{1}{2})$ & $(1,2)$ & 2 & right-handed Weyl $\tilde\psi_{\dal}$ & dotted \\
  $(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2})$ & $(2,2)$ & 4 & vector $A^\mu$ & spacetime \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\medskip

The key point for helicity: under a rotation by angle $\theta$ about the
momentum axis (the little group of a massless particle), a
$(\tfrac{1}{2},0)$ field picks up phase $e^{-i\theta/2}$ (helicity $-\tfrac{1}{2}$)
and a $(0,\tfrac{1}{2})$ field picks up $e^{+i\theta/2}$ (helicity $+\tfrac{1}{2}$).

%% ============================================================
\section{Left-Handed Weyl Spinors \texorpdfstring{$\psi_\alpha$}{psi\_alpha}}
%% ============================================================

A left-handed Weyl field $\psi_\alpha(x)$ lives in the $(\tfrac{1}{2},0)$
representation.  Under an infinitesimal Lorentz transformation
$\delta\omega_{\mu\nu}$:
\begin{equation}
  \delta\psi_\alpha = \tfrac{i}{2}\,\delta\omega_{\mu\nu}\,
    (S^{\mu\nu}_{\mathrm{L}})_\alpha{}^\beta\,\psi_\beta.
\end{equation}
The six generators are explicitly (Srednicki eqs.~34.9--34.10):
\begin{align}
  (S^{ij}_{\mathrm{L}})_\alpha{}^\beta &= \tfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon^{ijk}\sigma_k, &
  (S^{k0}_{\mathrm{L}})_\alpha{}^\beta &= \tfrac{i}{2}\sigma_k,
\end{align}
where $\sigma_k$ are the Pauli matrices:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_1 = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},\quad
  \sigma_2 = \begin{pmatrix}0&-i\\i&0\end{pmatrix},\quad
  \sigma_3 = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
These satisfy the Lorentz algebra $[S^{\mu\nu}_L, S^{\rho\sigma}_L]
= i(g^{\nu\rho}S^{\mu\sigma}_L - \cdots)$.  The boost generators
$S^{k0}_L$ are anti-Hermitian (the Lorentz group is non-compact),
reflecting that boosts are not unitary.

%% ============================================================
\section{Right-Handed Weyl Spinors \texorpdfstring{$\tilde\psi_{\dal}$}{psi-tilde}}
%% ============================================================

Hermitian conjugation swaps $N_i \leftrightarrow N_i^\dagger$, sending
$(\tfrac{1}{2},0) \to (0,\tfrac{1}{2})$.  So
\begin{equation}
  (\psi_\alpha)^\dagger \equiv \tilde\psi_{\dal}(x)
\end{equation}
is a \emph{right-handed} Weyl field with a \emph{dotted} index.
Its generators are (Srednicki eq.~34.17):
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{
    (S^{\mu\nu}_{\mathrm{R}})_{\dal}{}^{\dbe} = -[(S^{\mu\nu}_{\mathrm{L}})_\alpha{}^\beta]^*
  }
\end{equation}
Concretely this reverses the sign of boosts (consistent with
$L \leftrightarrow R$ under parity) and from eq.~(35.22):
\begin{equation}
  S^{\mu\nu}_{\mathrm{R}}
  = -\tfrac{i}{4}(\sbar^\mu\sigma^\nu - \sbar^\nu\sigma^\mu).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Convention note.}
We adopt Srednicki's metric $g^{\mu\nu} = \mathrm{diag}(+1,-1,-1,-1)$
and $\varepsilon^{12} = \varepsilon_{21} = +1$ throughout.
The amplitudes literature often uses the same conventions; we flag any
deviation when it appears.

%% ============================================================
\section{The \texorpdfstring{$\varepsilon$}{epsilon} Symbol:
  SL\texorpdfstring{$(2,\mathbb{C})$}{(2,C)} Metric}
%% ============================================================

From $({\tfrac12},0)\otimes({\tfrac12},0) = (0,0)_A\oplus(1,0)_S$
there is a unique (up to scale) invariant antisymmetric symbol.
With Srednicki's normalization (eq.~34.22 / 35.1):
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon^{12} = \varepsilon_{21} = +1, \qquad
  \varepsilon^{21} = \varepsilon_{12} = -1,
\end{equation}
so in matrix form:
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon_{\alpha\beta} = \begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
  \varepsilon^{\alpha\beta} = \begin{pmatrix}0&+1\\-1&0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad
  \varepsilon_{\alpha\beta}\varepsilon^{\beta\gamma} = \delta_\alpha{}^\gamma.
\end{equation}
The same structure holds for dotted indices.

\subsection{Raising and lowering}

\begin{align}
  \psi^\alpha &= \varepsilon^{\alpha\beta}\psi_\beta, &
  \psi_\alpha &= \varepsilon_{\alpha\beta}\psi^\beta.
\end{align}

\textbf{Sign trap (Srednicki eq.~34.27):}
\begin{equation}
  \psi^a\chi_a = \varepsilon^{ab}\psi_b\chi_a = -\varepsilon^{ba}\psi_b\chi_a = -\psi_b\chi^b.
\end{equation}
Hence $\psi^\alpha\chi_\alpha = -\psi_\alpha\chi^\alpha$: \emph{index
position matters and carries a sign}.

%% ============================================================
\section{Lorentz-Invariant Spinor Products}
%% ============================================================

The fundamental Lorentz-invariant bilinears are:
\begin{align}
  \psi\chi &\equiv \psi^\alpha\chi_\alpha
  = \varepsilon^{\alpha\beta}\psi_\alpha\chi_\beta, \label{eq:angle-bra}\\
  \tilde\psi\tilde\chi &\equiv \tilde\psi_{\dal}\tilde\chi^{\dal}
  = \varepsilon_{\dal\dbe}\tilde\psi^{\dal}\tilde\chi^{\dbe}. \label{eq:square-bra}
\end{align}
For Grassmann-valued fields, $\psi\chi = +\chi\psi$ (two minus signs cancel;
Srednicki eq.~35.25), and $(\psi\chi)^\dagger = \tilde\chi\tilde\psi$.

These are the precursors of the spinor brackets $\angleb{i}{j}$ and
$\squareb{i}{j}$ introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:spinor-helicity}: when
$\psi_\alpha = \lambda_{i\alpha}$ and $\chi_\alpha = \lambda_{j\alpha}$ are
the spinors of massless momenta $p_i$, $p_j$, then
$\angleb{i}{j} = \varepsilon^{\alpha\beta}\lambda_{i\alpha}\lambda_{j\beta}$
and $\squareb{i}{j} = \varepsilon_{\dal\dbe}\tilde\lambda_i^{\dal}\tilde\lambda_j^{\dbe}$.

%% ============================================================
\section{The \texorpdfstring{$\sigma^\mu$}{sigma\^{}mu} Symbol:
  Vector--Spinor Dictionary}
%% ============================================================

A field in the $(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2})$ representation carries one
undotted and one dotted index.  The dictionary to 4-vectors is
(Srednicki eq.~34.28--34.30):
\begin{equation}
  A_{\alpha\dal} = \sigma^\mu_{\alpha\dal}\,A_\mu, \qquad
  \sigma^\mu_{\alpha\dal} = (I,\,\vec\sigma).
\end{equation}
Explicitly:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^0 = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix},\;
  \sigma^1 = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},\;
  \sigma^2 = \begin{pmatrix}0&-i\\i&0\end{pmatrix},\;
  \sigma^3 = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Raising both spinor indices via the $\varepsilon$ symbols gives
(Srednicki eq.~35.19--35.20):
\begin{equation}
  \sbar^{\mu\,\dal\alpha} \equiv \varepsilon^{\alpha\beta}\varepsilon^{\dal\dbe}\,
  \sigma^\mu_{\beta\dbe} = (I,\,-\vec\sigma).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Key identities}

\begin{align}
  \sigma^\mu_{\alpha\dal}\,\sigma_{\mu\beta\dbe}
    &= -2\,\varepsilon_{\alpha\beta}\,\varepsilon_{\dal\dbe},
    \tag{35.4} \\
  \varepsilon^{\alpha\beta}\varepsilon^{\dal\dbe}\,
  \sigma^\mu_{\alpha\dal}\,\sigma^\nu_{\beta\dbe}
    &= -2\,g^{\mu\nu},
    \tag{35.5} \\
  \operatorname{tr}(\sigma^\mu\sbar^\nu) \equiv
  \sigma^\mu_{\alpha\dal}\,\sbar^{\nu\,\dal\alpha}
    &= 2\,g^{\mu\nu}, \\
  \sigma^\mu\sbar^\nu + \sigma^\nu\sbar^\mu
    &= -2\,g^{\mu\nu}\,I_2.
    \tag{35-Clifford}
\end{align}
The last line is the Weyl-spinor analogue of the Dirac Clifford algebra.
The generators are its antisymmetric part:
\begin{equation}
  S^{\mu\nu}_{\mathrm{L}} = \tfrac{i}{4}(\sigma^\mu\sbar^\nu - \sigma^\nu\sbar^\mu).
  \tag{35.21}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Momentum bi-spinor}

For any 4-momentum $p^\mu$, define
\begin{equation}
  p_{\alpha\dal} \equiv \sigma^\mu_{\alpha\dal}\,p_\mu
  = \begin{pmatrix} p^0+p^3 & p^1+ip^2 \\ p^1-ip^2 & p^0-p^3 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Then $\det(p_{\alpha\dal}) = (p^0)^2 - |\mathbf{p}|^2 = p^2$, so $p^2=0$
iff the matrix has rank 1 --- the factorization $p_{\alpha\dal}=\lambda_\alpha\tilde\lambda_{\dal}$
is equivalent to masslessness.  This is the starting point of
Chapter~\ref{ch:spinor-helicity}.

%% ============================================================
\section{Helicity from the Little Group}
\label{sec:helicity-little-group}
%% ============================================================

For a massless particle with momentum $p^\mu$, the little group (the
subgroup of the Lorentz group that fixes $p^\mu$) is $ISO(2)$,
whose compact part is $U(1)$.  Under the $U(1)$ rotation by angle
$\theta$, a field of helicity $h$ transforms as $e^{-2ih\theta}$.

In terms of spinors: the little-group generator rescales
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_\alpha \to e^{i\theta/2}\lambda_\alpha, \qquad
  \tilde\lambda_{\dal} \to e^{-i\theta/2}\tilde\lambda_{\dal},
\end{equation}
i.e.\ the continuous parameter $t = e^{i\theta/2}$ acts as
\begin{equation}
  \lambda \to t\lambda, \qquad \tilde\lambda \to t^{-1}\tilde\lambda.
  \label{eq:little-group-scaling}
\end{equation}
A state created by $\lambda_\alpha$ has helicity $-\tfrac{1}{2}$ (left-handed,
positive-chirality), and one created by $\tilde\lambda_{\dal}$ has
helicity $+\tfrac{1}{2}$ (right-handed, negative-chirality).

For a spin-1 gluon the helicity-$\pm 1$ polarization vectors scale as
$\varepsilon^\pm_\mu \to t^{\mp 2}\varepsilon^\pm_\mu$ under
\eqref{eq:little-group-scaling}; this determines the form of $\varepsilon^\pm$
in terms of spinors (see \S\ref{sec:spinor-helicity-polarization}).

%% ============================================================
\section{The Weyl Lagrangian}
\label{sec:weyl-lagrangian}
%% ============================================================

For a single left-handed Weyl field $\psi_\alpha$, the Lorentz-invariant
hermitian Lagrangian is (Srednicki eq.~36.2):
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{
    \mathcal{L}
    = i\tilde\psi_{\dal}\,\sbar^{\mu\,\dal\alpha}\,\partial_\mu\psi_\alpha
    - \tfrac{1}{2}m\,\varepsilon^{\alpha\beta}\psi_\alpha\psi_\beta
    - \tfrac{1}{2}m\,\varepsilon_{\dal\dbe}\tilde\psi^{\dal}\tilde\psi^{\dbe}.
  }
\end{equation}
The kinetic term is real modulo a total divergence.  The mass term is a
\emph{Majorana mass}: it breaks the $U(1)$ symmetry $\psi\to e^{i\alpha}\psi$
and is non-zero because $\psi_\alpha$ are Grassmann-valued.

\subsection{Equations of motion}

Varying with respect to $\tilde\psi^{\dal}$:
\begin{equation}
  -i\,\sbar^{\mu\,\dal\alpha}\,\partial_\mu\psi_\alpha + m\,\tilde\psi^{\dal} = 0.
  \tag{36.4}
\end{equation}
For $m=0$ this is the \textbf{Weyl equation} for a massless left-handed
spinor.  It is the field equation whose plane-wave solutions give
the spinors $\lambda_\alpha(p)$ used in amplitude calculations.

\subsection{Gamma matrices: Weyl representation}

Assembling $\psi_\alpha$ and $\tilde\psi_{\dal}$ into a 4-component
Majorana spinor $\Psi = (\psi_\alpha, \tilde\psi^{\dal})^T$, the equations
of motion take the Dirac form $(-i\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu + m)\Psi = 0$ with
(Srednicki eq.~36.7):
\begin{equation}
  \gamma^\mu =
  \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \sigma^\mu_{\alpha\dbe} \\
                  \sbar^{\mu\,\dal\beta} & 0 \end{pmatrix},
  \qquad
  \{\gamma^\mu,\gamma^\nu\} = -2g^{\mu\nu}I_4.
  \tag{36.7, 36.9}
\end{equation}
The chiral projectors are
$P_L = \tfrac{1}{2}(I-\gamma^5) = \mathrm{diag}(I_2,0)$ and
$P_R = \tfrac{1}{2}(I+\gamma^5) = \mathrm{diag}(0,I_2)$
with $\gamma^5 = i\gamma^0\gamma^1\gamma^2\gamma^3 = \mathrm{diag}(-I_2,+I_2)$.

%% ============================================================
\section{Majorana and Dirac Fermions}
%% ============================================================

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
  \toprule
  Property & Majorana & Dirac \\
  \midrule
  Weyl fields & 1 ($\psi$) & 2 ($\chi$, $\xi$) \\
  $U(1)$ symmetry & No & Yes \\
  Conserved charge & None & $Q = \int j^0\,d^3x$ \\
  Self-conjugate ($\Psi^C = \Psi$) & Yes & No \\
  Lagrangian & $\tfrac{i}{2}\bar\Psi\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu\Psi - \tfrac{m}{2}\bar\Psi\Psi$ &
               $i\bar\Psi\gamma^\mu\partial_\mu\Psi - m\bar\Psi\Psi$ \\
  Real d.o.f. & 2 & 4 \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

In amplitude calculations with external gluons (massless spin-1), the
internal spinors are always effectively massless Weyl spinors; the
Majorana/Dirac distinction matters for external fermion lines.
